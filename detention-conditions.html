Why Is This Happening?

We were told this was about removing dangerous criminals. It's not. So what's really driving the largest detention operation in American history?

Money.
Billions of dollars are flowing through this system. Here's how.
What Detainees Are Being Charged

People in detention—many with no income, no access to work—are being charged fees at every step.

$100
Each time you ask for more time to find a lawyer

$900

The American Immigration Council: These fees "apply even for people who are locked in ICE detention centers and have no access to money, essentially forcing people to proceed with their cases without any opportunity to get a lawyer."

In at least one documented case, an immigration judge ordered someone deported for nonpayment—before any mechanism to pay the fee even existed.

What They Get For That Money

They're being charged hundreds of dollars while living in conditions like this:

"Cells built for 66 people were crammed with up to 155. People slept head-to-foot on thin mats while others were held in cramped visitation rooms for over two weeks with only one exposed toilet."
— Human Rights Watch, Krome Detention Center, February-March 2025
"A cup of rice and a glass of water a day."
— Detainee account, Krome Detention Center, Miami, 2025
"Crammed into the perimeter of a very tiny room for up to 48 hours. They defecate in front of each other, they eat, they sleep on stone floors."
— Rep. Debbie Wasserman-Schultz after visiting Krome Detention Center, Miami, May 2025
"Shackled on buses for extended periods without food, water, or functioning toilets."
— Human Rights Watch, July 2025, documenting conditions at Krome, Broward Transitional Center, and Federal Detention Center (Miami)
"The only way I could describe it frankly is it's an absolute hell hole."
— Federal Judge Julie Robinson (Bush appointee) on CoreCivic's Leavenworth facility
