Why Accuracy Doesn't Matter to Fox's Bottom Line

Fox News could have zero advertising revenue and still maintain a 35% profit margin from carriage fees alone. Every cable subscriber pays ~$2.18/month to Fox News whether they watch or not.

Carriage fees are negotiated based on ratings and audience loyalty—not accuracy. The internal documents from the Dominion case revealed executives feared "viewers would be alienated and switch to rival conservative networks" if they reported the truth.

The incentive is clear: Outrage drives ratings. Ratings drive carriage fees. The lies made them money. The settlement was a speeding ticket.

Fox News's Own Legal Defense

"Any reasonable viewer arrives with an appropriate amount of skepticism about the statements [Tucker Carlson] makes."

— Fox News lawyers, successfully arguing Carlson's statements shouldn't be taken as fact (Case dismissed on these grounds, 2020)

The Bottom Line

Fox News isn't inherently bad for having a conservative perspective—many credible outlets do. The problem is a documented pattern of broadcasting information they knew to be false, paying historic settlements, and arguing in court that their hosts shouldn't be taken seriously.
